01 · Evaluation

Every position gets a score in centipawns (hundredths of a pawn). Positive favors White, negative favors Black. Material is the obvious part — but each piece also earns a bonus or penalty for where it stands, from a piece-square table, plus adjustments for pawn structure, the bishop pair, and mobility.

Pick a piece to paint its table onto the board and see what the engine actually values:

Teal = the engine likes this square for that piece. Red = it doesn't. This is why a knight parked in the corner gets penalized before anything else has even happened.

02 · Search: minimax + pruning

The engine plays out each candidate move, then assumes you'll reply with your best move, then it replies with its best move, and so on — White tries to maximize the score, Black tries to minimize it. That's minimax.

Once a branch is proven worse than a line already found, the engine stops reading it — that's alpha-beta pruning. It's the third branch above: whatever White finds there, the earlier line (+0.5) already beats it, so it's skipped instead of scored. It changes nothing about the result, only how fast it's reached.

03 · Difficulty = how far ahead it looks

Same engine at every level — the only thing that changes is how many moves ahead it's allowed to search, and how long it can think before answering:

Easy
2 plies · instant
Medium
3 plies · <0.1s
Hard
4 plies · up to ~0.8s
Master
5 plies · up to ~4s

Each extra ply means roughly 20–30× more positions to consider — that's why Master needs real time to think, and why it sees tactics a shallow search walks straight into. Against a stronger opponent, more depth is what closes the gap: it's the difference between spotting a trap next move and spotting one four moves away.

04 · Is this "deep learning"?

Not quite, on purpose. Engines like AlphaZero or Leela Chess Zero use a neural network trained across millions of self-played games, fused with tree search — but that trained network is a file hundreds of megabytes in size, built with serious training infrastructure. It can't be created from scratch inside one web page.

What's running here is classical search: minimax with alpha-beta pruning over the hand-built evaluation above. It's the same fundamental approach engines used for decades. The upside for a page like this: it's fully transparent. Every number in the candidates panel is a real score the engine computed — not a black box.

05 · Winning tricks — the tactical patterns to watch for

The engine names these live in the "Why this move" panel whenever they happen — fork, pin, skewer, discovered check, double check, back-rank mate, and smothered mate. The rest matter just as much in your own games, even though they unfold over a few moves rather than one detectable shape.

Fork
One piece attacks two or more enemy pieces at once — they can't all be saved.
Pin
A piece can't safely move because it's shielding a more valuable piece — often the king — directly behind it.
Skewer
A pin in reverse: the valuable piece is in front and forced to move, exposing the piece behind it to capture.
Discovered check
Moving one piece out of the way reveals check from a different piece behind it — the moving piece is free to do almost anything else at the same time.
Double check
Two pieces check the king simultaneously. Blocking or capturing can only stop one, so the king must move.
Back-rank mate
A king trapped on its own back rank by its own pawns gets mated by a rook or queen sliding along that rank.
Smothered mate
A king boxed in entirely by its own pieces gets mated by a knight, since it has nowhere to run and can't capture it.
Deflection
Forcing a defending piece away from the square or piece it's protecting, then exploiting the gap.
Zwischenzug
German for "in-between move" — inserting an unexpected, urgent threat before making an expected recapture.
Trapped piece
A piece with no safe square to retreat to. It can simply be won by attacking it once more than it's defended.
